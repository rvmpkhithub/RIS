Technique 2 of 3

Job To Be Done

Peeling back the stated task to find the real one underneath. Each layer looked like the answer — until the next peel revealed there was more to it.

Layer 1 · what he said he needs

Automate a manual daily image-sending task that currently eats a lot of his time.

Layer 2 · watching the manual process

Images must not repeat immediately for the same receiver.

Layer 3 · why that rule exists

Receivers notice — and complain — if they get repeat images too often. Variety matters to keep them satisfied.

Layer 4 · who these people actually are

Receivers are paying customers, not internal contacts. This is a subscription content business, not a personal broadcast tool.

The real job

Reliably fulfill a paid daily-image subscription automatically, with enough variety that paying customers don't churn or complain. The compliance hook may be less about the receiver's location and more about the app operator's own standing — license, payment — with whoever runs the compliance API.

Dashboard's real job: proof of delivery he can point to if a paying customer disputes "I didn't get my images."

📌 discovered need

Proof of delivery = a timestamp per image sent to each receiver, logged and viewable in the dashboard.

📌 discovered need

Min/max image count is configurable per receiver, not one global setting — different customers, different volume tiers.

Technique 3 of 3

⚡ Chaos Engineering

Break it on purpose. Eight failures injected on the system, one at a time — and one clean law fell out of how it kept surviving.

Connection drops, then reconnects mid-run.

Only resend that day's missed batch. Do not backfill prior missed days — those are handled manually.

An image is flagged inactive after being queued, but before it fires.

It still gets sent. Inactive only affects future selection, not what's already queued.

Active image count is less than the random Z chosen for the day.

Send however many are active. No duplicates, no topping up.

The compliance API call itself fails or times out.

Sending only stops on an explicit non-compliant response. If the check fails, just retry on the next scheduled run — and keep sending in the meantime.

↺ supersedes the 2-day cache rule from Technique 1

Picking the next image to send to a receiver.

Check that receiver's transmission queue/history first, so nothing already sent gets picked again.

The active image pool is too small to satisfy the 7-day no-repeat window, mathematically.

Repeats are allowed as a fallback.

A send attempt fails outright.

Retry 3 times regardless of the reason. Getting the receiver's number right is the friend's own responsibility.

OUT OF SCOPE ⚠ DO NOT INJECT HERE
Payment/billing between the friend and his customers is handled entirely offline — not by this app.
One law survived every test: fail open, not closed.

Only an explicit non-compliant response is ever allowed to stop sending. A short image pool, an unreachable compliance API, an exhausted no-repeat window — all of it degrades gracefully instead of blocking.

① The compliance hook is a licensing switch

It doubles as a remote install-licensing switch for the sideloaded app itself — independent of the friend-customer payments, which stay entirely out of scope.

Proof-of-delivery, the 7-day no-repeat check, and the 30-day dashboard aren't three separate features — they're all views over one transmissions ledger.
